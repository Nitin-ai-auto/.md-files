Equipment & Gear Essentials
---------------------------

Deductions for photography equipment and related gear

Camera & Equipment Purchases (Section 179 or Depreciation)

Cameras, lighting kits, laptops, and editing gear can be expensed via Section 179 or depreciated. Vermont conforms to federal Section 179 and bonus depreciation limits—up to $1.22M expensed immediately (2024).

$

Small Tools, Software & Accessories

Batteries, SD cards, editing software, and minor studio items are fully deductible in the year purchased. Vermont does not decouple from federal rules, so these expenses flow through unchanged.

$

Depreciation Records & Usage Allocation

Use IRS Form 4562 to track depreciation. Keep receipts and log business vs. personal use ratios. Vermont requires no addbacks—federal depreciation deductions carry into the state return.

$

Business Personal Property Tax

Vermont does not impose a statewide personal property tax on business assets. However, local municipalities may tax tangible property—check with your local clerk's office for filing thresholds or exemptions.

$

Studio/Office Expenses
----------------------

Deductions for studio and office expenses

Studio Rent & Utilities

If you rent a studio, rent and utilities (electricity, water, internet) are fully deductible. Vermont follows federal rules—expenses flow through from Schedule C to state income with no modification required.

$

Home Office Deduction

Photographers working from home can deduct a portion of rent, mortgage interest, utilities, and insurance. Vermont fully conforms to the federal home office deduction—use actual expenses or the simplified method ($5/sq ft).

$

Insurance Premiums

Business liability or property insurance premiums tied to your studio or home office are fully deductible. Vermont imposes no special restrictions—federal deductions apply to Vermont income directly.

$

Recordkeeping & IRS Compliance

Maintain utility bills, lease agreements, and IRS Form 8829 (if using actual expenses). Vermont does not require a separate state form—ensure your federal filing is correct and well documented.

$

Business Travel, Vehicles & On-the-Go Expenses
----------------------------------------------

Deductions for travel and transportation expenses

Mileage or Actual Vehicle Costs

Photographers can deduct either the IRS standard mileage rate or actual expenses (gas, maintenance, insurance). Vermont conforms to federal treatment—deductions taken on Schedule C reduce Vermont taxable income automatically.

$

Out-of-Town Business Travel

Airfare, hotel, rental car, and 50% of meals are deductible when incurred for business. Vermont mirrors federal rules—no special adjustments required at the state level for business-related travel costs.

$

Parking, Tolls & Local Transportation

Business-related tolls, parking fees, and rideshare or taxi costs are deductible. Vermont follows federal guidelines—these expenses pass through to your state return with no modifications.

$

Heavy Vehicle Depreciation (SUVs/Trucks)

If using a heavy SUV or truck over 6,000 lbs, federal depreciation limits apply. Vermont does not decouple from these limits—standard depreciation or Section 179 (within federal bounds) applies.

$

Documentation & Logs

Keep a mileage log, travel itineraries, and receipts. Vermont doesn’t require extra substantiation beyond federal standards—ensure records are accurate in case of audit.

$

Subcontractors, Assistants & Freelancers
----------------------------------------

Deductions for contracted labor

Independent Contractor Payments (1099-NEC)

Payments to second shooters, editors, or assistants are deductible as contract labor. Vermont follows federal Schedule C—amounts paid and reported on 1099-NEC reduce your Vermont taxable income.

$

W-9s and Recordkeeping Requirements

Collect W-9 forms from all independent contractors and issue 1099-NEC if payments exceed $600. Vermont requires no separate forms, but retains full audit rights—accurate recordkeeping is essential.

$

Employee Wages & Payroll Deductions

If hiring employees, wages and employer payroll taxes are deductible. Vermont recognizes these deductions automatically through federal AGI—ensure state withholding and unemployment accounts are properly registered.

$

Proper Worker Classification

Misclassifying employees as contractors can trigger penalties. Vermont adheres to federal classification tests—behavioral and financial control are key factors in audit determinations.

$

No Special Credits or Addbacks

Vermont offers no special credits for hiring subcontractors. Deductions for payments to legitimate workers flow through unadjusted unless audited for classification errors.

$

Training, Education & Development
---------------------------------

Deductions for training & professional development

Workshops, Courses & Continuing Education

Photography-related workshops, online classes, and industry conferences are deductible if they maintain or improve current skills. Vermont follows federal rules—these expenses reduce state income via adjusted gross income (AGI).

$

Professional Memberships & Trade Resources

Dues paid to professional groups like PPA or ASMP, and subscriptions to photography trade publications, are fully deductible. Vermont does not limit these write-offs if they qualify under federal business expense rules.

$

Travel for Educational Purposes

Travel costs tied to business education (e.g. conference flights, lodging) are deductible if the primary trip purpose is education. Vermont aligns with federal treatment—no state-specific limits or reporting changes.

$

Proof of Educational Relevance

Only education that relates to current business qualifies. Vermont audits use federal criteria—save receipts, syllabi, and confirmations to support the business relevance of any learning expense.

$

No Vermont-Specific Education Credits

Vermont does not offer business-specific tax credits for education. Deductions are standard Schedule C items, passed through from federal tax filings.

Marketing, Branding & Online Presence
-------------------------------------

Deductions for marketing & advertising

Online Ads, Print Materials & Promotions

Expenses for Facebook ads, Google Ads, flyers, brochures, and promotional prints are fully deductible. Vermont follows federal treatment—marketing costs reduce taxable income through your federal AGI.

$

Website Expenses & Branding Services

Website hosting, domain registration, SEO consulting, and logo design are business deductions. Vermont accepts all federally allowed promotional expenses without modification—document vendor invoices and usage.

$

Networking & Client Entertainment

Networking event tickets and small client meet-up costs (like coffee for project talks) are partially deductible. Vermont allows the standard federal deduction—50% for meals, and only if directly business-related.

$

Split Business & Personal Use Clearly

Mixed-use expenses (like trips for both business and personal content creation) must be prorated. Vermont uses federal allocation standards—only the business-use portion is deductible on your state return.

$

Digital Ad Taxes Not Applicable

Vermont currently imposes no separate gross revenue tax on digital ad platforms. You may deduct ad spend normally as long as it’s reported on your federal Schedule C.

Business Insurance, Licensing & Permits
---------------------------------------

Deductions for insurance & licenses

Business Insurance Premiums

Premiums for general liability, equipment insurance, E&O, and commercial auto policies are deductible. Vermont follows federal rules—deductions flow through from Schedule C without adjustment or cap.

$

Business License & Local Permit Fees

State or municipal licenses, local photo shoot permits, and registration fees are fully deductible. Vermont recognizes these fees as ordinary expenses, matching federal treatment—keep receipts for each filing or renewal.

$

Entity Formation & Annual Fees

LLC registration, trade name filings, and Vermont's $35 annual report fee (for LLCs) are deductible as startup or operating expenses. These flow through from federal calculations with no state-level disallowance.

$

Photography Sales & Use Tax Licenses

Photographers selling tangible or digital products must register for a Vermont sales tax license. Fees paid to obtain or maintain this permit are deductible—treat them as administrative costs on your return.

Props, Backdrops & Consumable Supplies
--------------------------------------

Deductions for supplies & consumables

Props, Backdrops & Styling Materials

Items like set decorations, furniture, backdrops, and client wardrobe are fully deductible if used exclusively for business. Vermont conforms to federal treatment—expense in the year purchased if not capitalized.

$

Consumables & Office Supplies

Memory cards, ink, paper, cleaning kits, printer stock, and shipping materials are ordinary business expenses. Vermont adopts federal deductibility rules—no special treatment or state-level adjustment applies.

$

Photo Albums, Prints & Delivery Materials

Client-facing goods such as albums, frames, USBs, and print packaging are deductible as cost of goods sold. Vermont allows standard federal treatment—track inventory and invoices for accuracy.

$

Sales Tax & Resale Certificate Use

To avoid double tax, use a Vermont resale certificate when buying items for resale (like albums or prints). Sales tax isn’t deductible but avoids upfront cost—maintain resale documentation.

$

No Additional Vermont Limitations

Vermont does not impose special caps or exclusions for props or supplies. Deduct all qualifying purchases per IRS rules and apply proper classification (supplies vs. COGS) for state compliance.

-------------------------------------------------

Deductions for financial expenses

Bank Charges & Merchant Account Fees

Monthly bank service fees, wire transfers, and card processing charges are fully deductible. Vermont conforms to federal treatment—include these ordinary costs on your return with no adjustment.

$

Interest on Business Loans & Credit Cards

Interest paid on business loans or business-use credit cards is deductible. Vermont adopts federal rules—track business-only use to ensure full deduction on both federal and state filings.

$

Financial Service Subscriptions & Charges

Fees for bookkeeping tools, accounting software, or invoicing platforms are deductible. Vermont imposes no special restrictions—deduct as ordinary necessary business services under federal guidelines.

$

Loan Documentation & Allocation Records

If a loan or card is used for both personal and business expenses, only the business-use portion is deductible. Vermont follows this allocation approach—maintain interest breakdowns and usage logs.

$

No Vermont-Specific Interest Limits

Vermont has not decoupled from federal interest deduction rules. Most small businesses are exempt from the federal 30% interest deduction cap, so Vermont imposes no additional limitation.

Other Vermont-Specific Deductions
---------------------------------

Other deductions specific to Vermont

Business Entity Filing Fees & Local Licensing

Vermont LLC and trade name registration fees are deductible. Annual reports ($35–$140) and DBA filings count as business expenses—fully deductible under federal and Vermont rules.

$

Sales Tax on Photo Products & Resale Certificates

Photo prints, albums, and tangible products are taxable in Vermont. Use a resale certificate to avoid paying sales tax when buying inventory. Services like sessions are generally not taxed.

$

Vermont Use Tax Compliance

If you buy gear out-of-state without sales tax, Vermont requires use tax payment. This tax is separate from income tax but must be paid. Keep invoices—noncompliance can trigger penalties.

$

Pass-Through Business Tax Election

Vermont allows pass-through businesses (LLCs, S-corps) to elect to pay state tax at the entity level. This may help high earners deduct state taxes federally. Discuss strategy with a CPA.

$

No Arts District Exemption

Unlike some states, Vermont does not offer income tax exemptions for artistic income from creative districts. All photography-related income is taxed under standard rules.
