What Are Debits and Credits in Accounting?
------------------------------------------

At its core, accounting is a system of tracking money as it flows in and out of a business. Every transaction has two sides: a debit and a credit.

*   Debit (Dr.): An entry on the left side of an account. It often represents an _increase in assets or expenses_ and a _decrease in liabilities or equity_.
*   Credit (Cr.): An entry on the right side of an account. It often represents an _increase in liabilities, equity, or revenue_ and a _decrease in assets or expenses_.

Think of it as a balancing act: for every debit, there must be an equal credit. This is the essence of double-entry accounting.

Example:

*   If you buy office supplies for $200 with cash:
    *   Debit: Office Supplies (+$200, asset increases)
    *   Credit: Cash (-$200, asset decreases)

The books remain balanced because the debit equals the credit.

Why Are Debits and Credits Important?
-------------------------------------

Debits and credits matter because they:

1.   Keep financial records accurate – Ensuring that every dollar spent or earned is accounted for.
2.   Support financial reporting – [Income statements](https://joinotto.com/templates/income-statement), [balance sheets](https://joinotto.com/templates/balance-sheet), and [cash flow statements](https://joinotto.com/templates/cash-flow-statement) all rely on accurate use of debits and credits.
3.   Prevent errors – Double-entry bookkeeping reduces the risk of mistakes since every entry must balance.
4.   Provide clarity for decision-making – Business owners and investors can see a clear picture of financial health.

In short, without debits and credits, accounting would be chaos.

The Golden Rules of Debit and Credit
------------------------------------

To understand how debits and credits work, accountants follow certain rules based on the type of account.

### Rule 1: Assets

*   Debit increases assets (cash, inventory, equipment, etc.)
*   Credit decreases assets

Example: Buying a laptop for your business.

*   Debit: Equipment (asset increases)
*   Credit: Cash (asset decreases)

### Rule 2: Liabilities

*   Debit decreases liabilities
*   Credit increases liabilities

Example: Taking out a loan for $5,000.

*   Debit: Cash (asset increases)
*   Credit: Loan Payable (liability increases)

### Rule 3: Equity

*   Debit decreases equity
*   Credit increases equity

Example: Owner invests $1,000 in the business.

*   Debit: Cash (asset increases)
*   Credit: Owner’s Equity (equity increases)

### Rule 4: Expenses

*   Debit increases expenses
*   Credit decreases expenses

Example: Paying $500 in rent.

*   Debit: Rent Expense (+$500)
*   Credit: Cash (-$500)

_(Answering the common question: Do you debit or credit expenses? → Expenses are debits.)_

### Rule 5: Revenue (Income)

*   Debit decreases revenue
*   Credit increases revenue

Example: Selling goods for $2,000.

*   Debit: Accounts Receivable (+$2,000 asset)
*   Credit: Sales Revenue (+$2,000 income)

Common Questions About Debits and Credits
-----------------------------------------

**1. Do you debit or credit expenses?**

Expenses are always debited because they reduce equity.

**2. Is equipment a debit or credit?**

Equipment is an asset, so increases are debited and decreases are credited.

**3. What’s the difference between debit and credit?**

*   Debit = left side, increases assets/expenses.
*   Credit = right side, increases liabilities/revenue/equity.

**4. Is revenue a debit or credit?**

Revenue is always a credit because it increases equity.

Debit and Credit Examples in Real Life
--------------------------------------

Let’s break down a few everyday transactions for a small business.

1.   Buying office supplies for $100 cash

*   Debit: Office Supplies (Asset +$100)
*   Credit: Cash (Asset -$100)

1.   Paying $1,000 for monthly rent

*   Debit: Rent Expense (Expense +$1,000)
*   Credit: Cash (Asset -$1,000)

1.   Taking a $5,000 bank loan

*   Debit: Cash (Asset +$5,000)
*   Credit: Loan Payable (Liability +$5,000)

1.   Selling services worth $2,500 on credit

*   Debit: Accounts Receivable (Asset +$2,500)
*   Credit: Service Revenue (Revenue +$2,500)

Each example follows the debit-credit rules perfectly.

Why Debits and Credits Confuse Beginners
----------------------------------------

Many beginners assume:

*   Debit = good, Credit = bad (like a debit card vs a credit card).

 But in accounting, both are neutral. They’re simply two sides of a balanced entry.

Tip to remember:

*   DEALER rule → Dividends, Expenses, Assets = Debit increases; Liabilities, Equity, Revenue = Credit increases.

The Role of Debits and Credits in Double-Entry Bookkeeping
----------------------------------------------------------

Double-entry bookkeeping means every financial transaction affects at least two accounts, one debit and one credit. This system:

*   Creates a self-checking balance.
*   Reduces fraud and error.
*   Ensures that the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) always holds true.

Debit vs Credit Accounting Example: Full Journal Entry
------------------------------------------------------

Imagine you run a bakery and purchase flour worth $500 on credit.

*   Debit: Inventory (Asset +$500)
*   Credit: Accounts Payable (Liability +$500)

Later, when you pay the supplier:

*   Debit: Accounts Payable (-$500 liability)
*   Credit: Cash (-$500 asset)

This shows the full cycle of a debit-credit relationship.
