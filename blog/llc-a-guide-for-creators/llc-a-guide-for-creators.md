**What is an LLC?**
-------------------

LLC stands for Limited Liability Company. It’s a flexible business structure that combines the pass-through taxation of a sole proprietorship or partnership with the limited liability protection of a corporation. In simple terms, it helps protect your personal assets while giving you options for managing taxes and ownership.

‍

**Key Features of an LLC:**

‍

*   **Separation of Assets:** Your personal assets are generally protected from business liabilities.
*   **Management Flexibility:** You can run your LLC yourself or with partners, with fewer formal requirements than a corporation.
*   **Tax Options:** By default, single-member LLCs are taxed as sole proprietorships, but you can elect corporate taxation if beneficial.

‍

--------------------------

One of the main reasons creators consider forming an LLC is protection from liability. If your business faces lawsuits or debt, your personal assets—like your home, savings, or car—are generally shielded from claims.

**Steps to Form an LLC**
------------------------

If you decide that forming an LLC makes sense, here’s a general roadmap:

‍

1.   **Choose a Name:** Make sure it’s unique and complies with state regulations.
2.   **File Articles of Organization:** Submit the necessary paperwork to your state.
3.   **Create an Operating Agreement:** Outline ownership, roles, and responsibilities.
4.   **Obtain Licenses and Permits:** Ensure your business is legally compliant.
5.   **Get an EIN:** Apply for an Employer Identification Number from the IRS.
6.   **Open a Business Bank Account:** Keep your personal and business finances separate.

**Alternatives to an LLC**
--------------------------

An LLC is just one of several business structures. Depending on your needs, consider these alternatives:

‍

*   **Sole Proprietorship:** Simple to set up but offers no personal liability protection.
*   **Partnership:** For businesses with multiple owners, but partners share liability.
*   **S Corporation:** Offers some tax advantages but has stricter rules.
*   **C Corporation:** More complex, generally used for larger businesses.
